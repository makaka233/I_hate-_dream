Redundant Staged Service Placement and Task
Offloading Optimization in Collaborative Edge
Computing: A World-Model-KKT Approach
Anonymous Author(s)
Abstract—With the rapid development of 5G/6G, the Internet
of Things, industrial Internet, and edge intelligence, an increasing
number of latency-sensitive applications require collaborative
processing over multiple edge nodes. In such scenarios, complex
services are no longer monolithic; instead, they are increasingly
decomposed into multiple ordered processing stages. This trend
leads to a coupled optimization problem involving staged service
placement, stage-level task offloading, node computing resource
allocation, and inter-edge link bandwidth allocation. To address
this issue, this paper establishes a unified optimization model
for collaborative edge computing and minimizes the total end-
to-end system delay under service placement, task offloading,
node capacity, and link bandwidth constraints. Considering that
service placement and task offloading are discrete decisions, while
computing and bandwidth allocation are continuous variables, we
further transform the original mixed-integer nonlinear problem
into a hierarchical framework. At the upper level, a world-
model-driven hierarchical decision framework determines service
placement and task offloading decisions. At the lower level, after
fixing the discrete decisions, the continuous resource allocation
problem is decomposed into convex node-level and link-level
subproblems, and the closed-form optimal solutions are derived
via the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions. Specifically,
the optimal node computing resources and link bandwidths
are shown to follow square-root proportional allocation rules.
Based on these results, a world-model-KKT collaborative solution
framework is developed, which improves the efficiency of upper-
level decision generation while preserving the global optimality
of the lower-level continuous resource allocation.
Index Terms—Edge computing, staged services, service place-
ment, task offloading, DRL, KKT, resource allocation.
I. INTRODUCTION
WITH the rapid development of 5G/6G, the Internet of
Things (IoT), industrial Internet, and edge intelligence,
applications such as augmented reality, intelligent manufactur-
ing, Internet of Vehicles, urban sensing, and real-time video
analytics have imposed increasingly stringent requirements on
low latency, high reliability, and localized computing capabil-
ity. Compared with the conventional cloud-centric paradigm
that forwards all service requests to remote data centers, mo-
bile edge computing (MEC) pushes computation and storage
capabilities to the network edge and thus provides a promising
solution for low-latency service provisioning [1], [11].
Meanwhile, the service paradigm at the edge is evolving
from monolithic applications to fine-grained and orchestratable

service structures. Service placement, task offloading, and
resource allocation have therefore become core issues in edge
computing. On the one hand, service instances should be
placed over resource-limited edge nodes. On the other hand,
user tasks should be offloaded to appropriate nodes for pro-
cessing, while computing and communication resources must
be coordinated accordingly [2], [3]. For complex applications,
service execution is often sequential and stage-dependent.
Once a service is decomposed into multiple ordered stages,
the system must determine not only the placement of each
sub-service, but also the execution node of each task stage,
as well as the corresponding node computing resources and
inter-edge link bandwidths for cross-node stage transitions.
Under this setting, the considered problem faces four main
challenges. First, service placement and task offloading are
strongly coupled. A task stage can only be processed by a
node that has already placed the corresponding sub-service.
Hence, placement decisions directly determine the feasible
offloading set, while the offloading results in turn affect
node workloads and the placement benefit [4]–[6]. Second,
computing resources and communication resources are jointly
constrained. When adjacent stages are processed at different
nodes, additional inter-edge transmission delay is introduced
and becomes tightly coupled with node-level computing delay
[3], [8]. Third, different decision variables naturally operate at
different update frequencies. Service placement usually incurs
container preparation and migration overhead and is therefore
unsuitable for frequent updates, whereas task offloading and
resource allocation should react to task arrivals in a much
shorter timescale [2]. Fourth, the original optimization prob-
lem contains both binary and continuous variables and exhibits
clear mixed-integer nonlinear characteristics, which makes
direct end-to-end optimization or direct DRL over the entire
action space highly challenging [2], [11].
To address the above issues, this paper investigates redun-
dant staged service placement and stage-level task offloading
in collaborative edge computing. We first build a unified model
that jointly describes sub-service placement, stage-level task
offloading, node computing resource allocation, and inter-
edge link bandwidth allocation. Then, by minimizing the total
end-to-end system delay, we formulate a joint optimization
problem under placement, offloading, computing, bandwidth,
memory, and storage constraints. Considering the heteroge-
neous nature of the decision variables, we adopt a hierarchical
strategy: the upper level uses DRL to determine staged service
placement and task offloading decisions, whereas the lower

level derives the optimal continuous resource allocation by
KKT after the discrete decisions are fixed.
The main contributions of this paper are summarized as
follows.
1) We establish a unified optimization model for col-
laborative edge computing with staged services. The
model jointly considers redundant sub-service place-
ment, stage-level task offloading, node computing re-
source allocation, and inter-edge link bandwidth alloca-
tion.
2) We rigorously transform the continuous resource allo-
cation part into convex subproblems and derive closed-
form optimal solutions based on the KKT conditions.
The resulting expressions show that both node comput-
ing resources and link bandwidth are optimally allocated
according to square-root proportional rules.
3) We develop a world-model-KKT hierarchical solution
framework. The upper layer uses a deployment world
model and a scheduler world model to generate discrete
placement and offloading decisions, while the lower-
level KKT module provides globally optimal continu-
ous resource allocation under fixed discrete decisions,
thereby improving both tractability and theoretical rigor.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Sec-
tion II reviews the most relevant studies in the past three
years and presents a comparison table. Section III establishes
the system model and formulates the joint optimization prob-
lem. Section IV transforms the original problem, derives the
optimal continuous resource allocation based on KKT, and
develops the world-model-KKT algorithmic framework.
II. RELATED WORK
A. Service Placement and Request Routing
In the recent literature on edge service orchestration, one
important line of research focuses on the joint optimization of
service placement and request routing. Peng et al. studied the
joint optimization of service deployment and request routing
for microservices in MEC and demonstrated the significant
coupling between placement locations and routing strategies
[4]. Hu et al. further incorporated multi-objective DRL into
microservice deployment and routing over collaborative edge
environments, revealing that service granularity and service
dependency can significantly affect both deployment gain
and transmission overhead [5]. Peng et al. also investigated
fine-grained microservice deployment and routing with delay
awareness, which further pushed the research frontier from
coarse service instances to more refined service structures [6].
These studies provide important insights for our work:
service placement should not be considered independently
of request or task routing; fine-grained service dependen-
cies substantially affect communication overhead; and static
optimization is often insufficient in dynamic environments,
which motivates the use of online learning or reinforcement
learning. Nevertheless, most of these studies mainly focus
on microservice deployment and routing, rather than explicit
stage-level task execution with analytical continuous resource
allocation.
B. Joint Optimization of Service Placement, Task Offloading,
and Resource Allocation
Another line of work more closely related to our study is
the joint optimization of service placement, task offloading,
and resource allocation. Fan et al. jointly considered service
placement, task scheduling, computing resource allocation,
and transmission rate allocation in edge-cloud collaborative
networks and coordinated the solution process across different
timescales [3]. More recently, Fan et al. proposed a dual-agent
DRL framework for multi-sensor and multi-user edge com-
puting networks, where service placement, task scheduling,
transmit power allocation, computing resource allocation, and
transmission rate allocation are jointly optimized in a task-
data-decoupled setting [2].
These works clearly indicate that, as edge systems become
increasingly complex, optimizing a single type of decision
variable in isolation is generally insufficient. Instead, a system-
level view is required to jointly consider service placement,
task offloading, and resource allocation. Existing joint op-
timization works are mostly request-centric, task-centric, or
task-data-decoupled. In contrast, the present study explicitly
models the sequential processing chain of staged services and
derives the optimal continuous resource allocation in a closed
form under stage-dependent service execution.
C. Dependency-Aware Microservice Modeling and Dependent
Task Offloading
As edge applications evolve from monolithic services to
microservices and multi-stage processing chains, service de-
pendency and task dependency have attracted increasing atten-
tion. Wang et al. proposed a dependency-aware microservice
placement scheme using network representation and DRL,
which improves the placement quality of dependent microser-
vices [7]. Lou et al. studied dependent task scheduling under
bandwidth constraints and startup-awareness, which is highly
relevant to stage-dependent execution in edge environments
[8]. Dai et al. investigated the offloading of dependent tasks
with unknown system-side information, showing the impor-
tance of considering dependency structures when system-side
conditions are uncertain [9].
These studies demonstrate that dependency structures are
already a key issue in edge computing research. Nonetheless,
from the modeling perspective, existing works mainly focus on
either dependency-aware microservice placement or dependent
task scheduling/offloading. They rarely integrate dependency
structure, service placement, stage-level offloading, and ana-
lytically derived continuous resource allocation into a unified
framework. This gap motivates the study carried out in this
paper.

computing because it can learn near-optimal decisions through
environmental interaction even when the system model is com-

E. Summary of Existing Studies and Positioning of This Work
Overall, the representative studies from 2023–2025 have
achieved substantial progress in service placement, request
routing, task offloading, dependency-aware modeling, and
DRL-based decision making. However, three limitations re-
main. First, most studies still focus on request routing or
task offloading instead of explicitly modeling a staged service
execution chain. Second, although some works jointly consider
service placement, task offloading, and resource allocation, the
structural decomposition between discrete decisions and con-
tinuous resource variables remains insufficient. Third, existing
multi-timescale methods generally rely on numerical or black-
box treatments for continuous variables, rather than rigorously
deriving closed-form optimal solutions.
Accordingly, this paper focuses on redundant staged service
placement and stage-level task offloading in collaborative edge
computing. The relationship between the present study and

A. Network Architecture and Staged Service Description
We consider a collaborative edge computing system with
M edge nodes, where each edge node is equipped with one
edge server. The set of all edge nodes is denoted by M =
{1, . . . , M}. For any two edge nodes Em and Em′ , a binary
topology indicator am,m′ is introduced to describe whether
the two nodes are directly connected. When am,m′ = 1,
the two nodes can communicate through an inter-edge link
whose maximum available transmission rate is Rm,m′ . When
am,m′ = 0, direct data transmission between the two nodes
is not allowed, and therefore no direct cross-stage forwarding
can be performed over that pair of nodes.
Let Nm denote the number of users covered by edge node
Em, and let Um,n denote the nth user associated with edge
node Em. Each user submits service requests to its affiliated
edge node first, and then the request may continue to be
processed locally or be forwarded to other edge nodes accord-
ing to the staged service placement and stage-level offloading
decisions. This paper considers a set of service types I =
{1, . . . , I}. Since complex applications are generally executed
in multiple ordered processing stages, service i is decomposed
into Ji staged sub-services, denoted by Si,1, Si,2, . . . , Si,Ji .
Correspondingly, for the request of service i generated by user
Um,n, the stage-j task is denoted by Km,n,i,j.
The above modeling means that each user request of service
i is no longer processed in one monolithic step. Instead, it
forms an ordered execution chain Km,n,i,1 → Km,n,i,2 →
· · · → Km,n,i,Ji
. The output of one stage becomes the input of
the next stage. If two adjacent stages are executed on different
edge nodes, the intermediate result produced by the previous
stage must be transmitted through an inter-edge link before
the next stage can start. Therefore, the end-to-end latency of
a request depends jointly on the placement locations of the
staged sub-services, the offloading destinations of each task
stage, the node computing resources allocated to each active
stage, and the link bandwidth allocated to each inter-node
transmission.

G. Problem Formulation
The optimization goal of this paper is to minimize the total
system delay by jointly determining staged service placement,
stage-level task offloading, node computing resource alloca-
tion, and inter-edge transmission rate allocation. For compact
description, define the placement-variable set X = {xi,j,m′ },
the offloading-variable set Y = {ym,n,i,j,m′ }, the computing-
allocation set F = {fm′,m,n,i,j}, and the link-rate-allocation
set R = {rm,n,i,0,m,m′ , rm,n,i,j,m′,m′′ }. Based on the fully ex-

IV. PROBLEM TRANSFORMATION AND ALGORITHM
DESIGN
In Section III, the original optimization problem P1 has
been established with four types of decision variables: service
placement x, task offloading y, node computing resource
allocation f, and inter-edge link rate allocation r. Since x
and y are binary variables whereas f and r are continuous
variables, directly solving P1 is difficult. To address this
issue, this section transforms P1 into a hierarchical solution
framework. The upper layer uses DRL to determine x and y,
while the lower layer optimizes f and r under fixed discrete
decisions. As will be shown below, the lower-layer problem
can be decomposed into convex subproblems whose global
optima can be obtained via the KKT conditions.

D. Overall Algorithm Design
1) World-Model-KKT Hierarchical Solution Framework:
Based on the above derivations, the original problem P1 can
be transformed into a two-level solution framework. At the
upper level, a DRL agent outputs service placement decisions
x and task offloading decisions y. At the lower level, x and y
are treated as inputs, and the optimal node computing resource
allocation f∗
and optimal link bandwidth allocation r∗
are
computed directly by (46), (57), and (58). Since the lower-
level solution is obtained in closed form, no additional iterative
search is needed, and the global optimality of continuous re-
source allocation under fixed discrete decisions is guaranteed.
Moreover, the node-level minimum computing delay is
given by (47), while the link-level minimum transmission
delay is given by (59). Therefore, the continuous variables
f and r can be analytically eliminated from the original
formulation, and their optimal responses can be fed back to
the upper-level world-model modules for action scoring, plan-
ner distillation, and policy optimization, which significantly
reduces the complexity of the joint optimization process.
2) Algorithm Procedure: The overall World-Model-KKT
procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1.
Theorem 3. Under fixed upper-level placement decisions x
and offloading decisions y, the continuous resource alloca-
tion pair (f∗
, r∗
) given by (46), (57), and (58) satisfies all
KKT conditions of the corresponding convex subproblems and
therefore constitutes the global optimum of the lower-level
continuous resource allocation problem.
In summary, the proposed world-model-KKT hierarchical
framework effectively transforms the original mixed-integer
nonlinear optimization problem into a collaborative architec-
ture consisting of world-model-driven placement and offload-
ing generation, and KKT-based analytical optimization for
continuous resource allocation. The upper-level decision pro-
cess improves the efficiency of discrete action generation and
evaluation, whereas the lower-level KKT solution guarantees
the optimality of continuous resource allocation under fixed
discrete decisions, thereby improving both solution efficiency
and theoretical rigor.
